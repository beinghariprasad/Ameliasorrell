### A5. Preschooler Interest Map Template (Ages 4–6)

Instructions (Chapter 5):

1. Write your child's name in the center circle
2. Surround it with bubbles — one for each strong interest
3. Make bigger bubbles for stronger interests
4. Look for clusters: Are most bubbles physical? Creative? Social? Investigative?

```
              [Interest]     [Interest]
                   \           /
        [Interest] — [CHILD'S NAME] — [Interest]
                   /           \
              [Interest]     [Interest]
```

Update this monthly. Compare maps over time to see what's growing and what's fading.

### A10. The J/N/D Activity Audit

A quick exercise for evaluating your child's current schedule (Chapter 9).

| Activity | Child's feeling: J (Joy) / N (Neutral) / D (Dread) | Your load: E (Easy) / H (Hard) | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| | | | |
| | | | |
| | | | |
| | | | |
| | | | |

**Decision guide:**
- **J + E** = Keep. This is the sweet spot.
- **J + H** = Keep if possible. Look for ways to reduce the burden on you.
- **N + E** = Keep for now, but monitor. If it doesn't become J, consider dropping.
- **N + H** = Have a conversation. This may not be worth the cost.
- **D + anything** = Stop. Immediately. Joy matters more than sunk costs.

## D. About the Author

I didn't plan to write this book.

I was a classroom teacher for years, and I was fine at it. Good, even. But the thing that kept pulling at me wasn't what happened during my lessons. It was what happened *around* them. A kid who couldn't focus during reading time but built the most detailed Lego city I'd ever seen during free period. A girl who barely spoke in class but ran the entire social world of the playground at recess. A boy who drove his parents crazy taking apart every electronic in the house, and all I could think was, *someone needs to hand that kid a circuit board.*

I kept seeing kids whose best stuff was invisible to the systems measuring them.

So I left the classroom. I trained in developmental psychology. I got certified in early childhood education. And then I did the thing that taught me more than any of it: I started talking to parents.

Thousands of them, over the years. In workshops, in coaching sessions, in coffee shops, and in rushed five-minute conversations at school pickup. They were tired. They were doing their best. And almost every single one of them asked me some version of the same question: *"Am I missing something with my kid?"*

That question is why this book exists.

I'm a child development specialist, a parenting educator, and a curriculum designer. I've built programs for schools, community organizations, and families who just wanted someone to give it to them straight. I don't do jargon. I don't do scare tactics. I do practical, honest guidance that fits into the kind of life where you're also trying to remember if you switched the laundry.

I should also be honest about something: I am not a perfect parent. My own kids have watched too much TV on days when I was fried. I've praised talent when I should have praised effort. I've signed them up for things *I* wanted them to do instead of paying attention to what *they* were drawn to. Everything in this book, I learned the messy way first.

But I watch my kids now. Really watch them. And the things I see, when I'm quiet enough to notice, still surprise me.

I hope this book helps you see those things, too.

**— Amelia Sorrell**
